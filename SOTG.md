# Summary

**Spirit of the Game** is ultimate’s central fair-play principle. It means that players are not only athletes but also active guardians of the rules, the tone of the game, and the safety of opponents. WFDF’s official rules say that ultimate places responsibility for fair play on every player, trusting that players will not intentionally break the rules.

The key idea is: **ultimate is competitive, but self-controlled**. High-level, intense play is encouraged, but not at the expense of respect, safety, honesty, or enjoyment. WFDF’s historical description of Spirit explicitly says that competitive play should never override mutual respect or the basic joy of play.

In practical terms, Spirit means players should:

|Area|What it means|
|---|---|
|**Rules knowledge**|Players should know the rules well enough to make and discuss calls fairly.|
|**Honesty**|Players should call fouls, violations, goals, and out-of-bounds situations truthfully, even when it hurts their own team.|
|**Respectful communication**|Disagreements should be calm, brief, and based on what players actually saw.|
|**Self-control**|Players should avoid dangerous play, intimidation, revenge calls, or emotional escalation.|
|**Fair competition**|Trying hard is good; cheating, exploiting ignorance, or deliberately fouling is not.|

Because ultimate is usually **self-officiated**, Spirit is not just “being nice.” It is the mechanism that allows the sport to function without referees. USA Ultimate describes Spirit as placing responsibility for fair play on the athletes themselves, requiring players to know the rules and make their own calls without neutral officials.

A common misunderstanding is that Spirit means avoiding conflict or being lenient. It does **not**. Good Spirit can include making firm calls, contesting incorrect calls, or correcting an opponent’s misunderstanding. The important part is that calls are made honestly, explained clearly, and resolved respectfully.

Disputes are handled by discussion between the involved players. If they agree, play continues according to that outcome. If they do not agree, the rules usually provide a reset mechanism, often returning the disc to the previous thrower or resuming in the way that best reflects what likely would have happened without the disputed incident. WFDF frames this as restoring play rather than punishing breaches harshly.

At many tournaments, Spirit is also **measured**. WFDF describes a Spirit Scoring system where teams rate opponents, and often themselves, after games across five fundamentals of the sport. This makes Spirit something teams can discuss, improve, and be held accountable for, not just an abstract ideal.

Many teams also use **Spirit circles** after games: both teams stand together, often alternating players, to acknowledge positives and discuss issues that came up. WFDF presents these circles as a way to connect with opponents and resolve conflict constructively.

In one sentence: **Spirit of the Game is the expectation that ultimate players compete hard while jointly protecting fairness, safety, respect, and the integrity of self-officiation.**
